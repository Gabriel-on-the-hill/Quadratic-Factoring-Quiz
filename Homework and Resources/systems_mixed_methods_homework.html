Part 1 — Study these examples (every step names the equation)

Substitution Use when a variable is already isolated.

System: \( \begin{cases} y = 3x - 5 & \text{equation i} \\ 2x + y = 10 & \text{equation ii} \end{cases} \)

Step 1 Action: Substitute the expression from equation i into equation ii. So, work on ii: \( 2x + (3x - 5) = 10 \)
Step 2 Action: Simplify and solve equation ii for x. \( 5x - 5 = 10 \Rightarrow 5x = 15 \Rightarrow x = 3 \)
Step 3 Action: Plug the found value of x into equation i to find y. Work on i: \( y = 3(3) - 5 = 9 - 5 = 4 \)
Result: Solution is \( (3,4) \). Note how each step explicitly states which equation is being used or changed.
Elimination Use when variables line up and you can cancel one.

System: \( \begin{cases} 2x + 9y = -1 & \text{equation i} \\ 3x + 3y = -5 & \text{equation ii} \end{cases} \)

Step 1 Action: Multiply equation ii by \( -3 \) so the coefficient of y becomes \( -9 \), which will cancel with equation i's \(9y\). New equation (ii'): \( -9x - 9y = 15 \)
Step 2 Action: Add equation i and the new equation ii' to eliminate y and solve for x. \( (2x + 9y) + (-9x - 9y) = -1 + 15 \Rightarrow -7x = 14 \Rightarrow x = -2 \)
Step 3 Action: Substitute x = -2 into one of the original equations — we will use equation ii to solve for y. Work on ii: \( 3(-2) + 3y = -5 \Rightarrow -6 + 3y = -5 \Rightarrow 3y = 1 \Rightarrow y = \tfrac{1}{3} \)
Result: Solution is \( (-2,\tfrac{1}{3}) \). Each step clearly names which equation you're transforming or using to substitute.
